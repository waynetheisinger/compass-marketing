The situation in the Strait of Hormuz has reached a critical stage as of mid-April 2026, significantly impacting global energy costs and consumer behavior within the garden machinery sector. [1, 2] 
## Current Situation in the Strait of Hormuz

* Naval Blockade: Following the collapse of peace talks, the U.S. has initiated a naval blockade of Iranian ports and coastal areas.
* Shipping Disruption: Commercial transit has collapsed by approximately 94%, with over 3,200 vessels—including 800 tankers—stalled or rerouted.
* Military Conflict: The blockade follows weeks of military strikes by the U.S. and Israel against Iranian nuclear and energy infrastructure, and retaliatory Iranian strikes across the region.
* Supply Shortfall: Roughly 20 million barrels of oil per day have been affected, creating a global deficit that has pushed crude prices back above $100 per barrel. [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10] 

## Impact on Consumer Confidence & Garden Machinery
The conflict is reshaping the "petrol products" segment of the Garden Machinery sector through price shocks and supply chain volatility.

* Petrol Price Spikes: In April 2026, retail gasoline prices have surged to monthly averages of $4.30 per gallon in the U.S., while European prices have risen by over 20%. This immediately increases the operating cost of petrol-powered lawnmowers, chainsaws, and brush cutters.
* Cost-Driven Retreat: Surging fuel costs are dampening household spending on non-essential travel and services, with consumers becoming increasingly wary of large petrol-based purchases.
* Accelerated Electrification: The energy shock is forcing a "painful and quick" transition toward electric alternatives. Consumer preference is shifting rapidly toward battery-powered (cordless) garden tools to avoid volatile fuel prices and noise/emission restrictions.
* Manufacturing Surcharges: Because energy and petrochemicals are embedded in raw materials and packaging, manufacturers are applying surcharges of up to 30%, further eroding consumer confidence through higher retail price tags.
* Fragile Recovery: Although a temporary ceasefire briefly lowered prices below $100, the subsequent blockade has kept market confidence fragile and prices elevated, suggesting prolonged difficulties for retailers of traditional petrol equipment. [4, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18]

Nationally, the garden machinery sector is seeing a distinct price divergence as the conflict in the Strait of Hormuz drives up fuel and manufacturing costs. [1, 2] 
## National Price Landscapes (April 2026)
Consumer confidence is being squeezed by both the immediate cost of petrol at the pump—which has surged following the naval blockade—and the higher entry price of advanced battery technology. [3, 4] 

* Entry-Level (Push Mowers):
* Petrol: Basic models generally start between £130 and £190.
   * Cordless/Battery: Entry-level kits for small lawns typically range from £120 to £180.
* Mid-Range (Self-Propelled):
* Petrol: Standard 46cm models from national brands average £250 to £350.
   * Cordless/Battery: Equivalent battery kits with sufficient power for medium lawns are currently priced higher, typically between £350 and £500.
* Premium/Professional:
* Petrol: High-torque models for large or rough terrain can range from £550 to over £1,000.
   * Cordless/Battery: Professional-grade systems often exceed £1,000, with some specialized units reaching £1,700+. [5, 6, 7, 8, 9] 

## Confidence and Cost Shifts

* Fuel Inflation: With crude oil exceeding $100 per barrel, the annual cost to fuel a petrol mower is now estimated to be significantly higher than in previous years, which is deterring long-term investment in petrol products.
* The "Upfront" Barrier: Despite rising fuel costs, consumer confidence in transitioning to electric is tempered by the 15-20% higher upfront cost for premium cordless tools.
* Maintenance Savings: Expert reviews from [Which?](https://www.which.co.uk/reviews/lawn-mowers/article/how-to-buy-the-best-lawn-mower-aGhEi1V0rGcC) and [Gardeners' World](https://www.gardenersworld.com/reviews/lawn-care/best-lawn-mowers/) increasingly highlight that battery mowers can save hundreds of pounds over five years by eliminating the £100-£200 annual servicing and fuel costs associated with petrol engines.
